# Opener workshop — 2026-07-15

> Live working session with the director, applying the infomercial
> grammar from Snyder's "How to describe your product (quickly)" (the
> Turbo Pump breakdown) to settle the opener and closer. Everything the
> 2026-07-12 package left as "candidates for ruling" got workshopped
> here; this doc records what was ruled, what was killed, and what still
> needs a live test. Voice guardrails still bind every word.

## The grammar (what the Turbo Pump teaches)

1. **0:00–0:05 — PULL.** One razor-specific moment, in the buyer's
   second person. Never open broad; the broad frame is *earned* at
   0:16, never stated at 0:00.
2. **0:05–0:10 — supply that fits.** Product name + category + the
   three words that say "accomplish X without Y."
3. **0:10–0:16 — how it fits.** When the buyer already knows the
   incumbent category, this is *contrast*, not process (the Breathe
   Easy humidifier move).
4. **0:30 — the mechanism.** Where minority-felt insights live. Nobody
   buys because of the Turbo Syphon; it's why they believe it'll work.
5. **Fan-out.** "Other times you have this pull" — wiper fluid,
   radiator, snowblower. This is where our persona list goes.
6. **Closer.** Jog the memory → reverse the risk → push to act.

### Method rules established

- **Big→Small selects the opener; it never sequences the pitch.**
  "Big" = the concrete moment that happens to the most people, most
  often, most painfully — not the highest-altitude explanation.
  Opening abstract is the founder antipattern quadrant.
- **Open only on a remembered burn or a self-evident truth.** If the
  opener has to educate the buyer about the threat before they feel
  it, it's push wearing pull's clothes.
- **Minority-felt insights go in the mechanism slot, never the
  opener.** They're differentiators for people already nodding.

## The seed: the founder story (Alexandria)

The commission: a partner asked for "a really good version" of a
17-step skill (an agent builds atomic libraries out of source
materials), landing in the product in front of every user, multiple
times. The plan was ~4 bundles.

The cobble that made the founder "melt": 4 tabs each producing
artifacts → shipped to Braintrust → tracked *there* → brought back →
bundles looped together → intersection tests → dogfood in the product →
a spreadsheet and a notepad reconciling three surfaces → plus a fifth
tab running an agent thought-partner just to decide *what evals to even
build*, because the eval bench gives you a bench and no process.

The punchline (kept as internal origin story, killed as ad copy — see
kills): it was faster to build a unified system to manage one skill
than to build that skill "naked."

The recurring burn: **1–2 hours a week hunting for work already done**
— skills lost in orphaned branches and unnoticed PRs, "which tab, which
room did that research come from." Second witness: the founder's
partner's evals company — ~30% of the job is schlep across four
workstations that don't shake hands; skills fork and drift by accident.
More cooks, worse infrastructure.

### The commission PULL grid

- **P:** "Get me a really good version of this skill" — commissioned by
  someone you answer to; "really good" is their bar.
- **U:** it lands in front of every user, repeatedly; a partner is
  waiting; failure is a visible, recurring product defect.
- **L (looking):** the cobble above — tabs, an eval bench in another
  surface, spreadsheet/notepad reconciliation, hand-rolled process.
- **L (lacking):** tracking becomes half the job; the eval copy
  diverges from the shipped copy; and the likeliest failure —
  *procedural, stable-but-wrong* — is invisible to all of it.

This **rehabilitates the buyer project underneath dead vector 6**. The
07-12 demotion was right about the supply-side framing ("author a skill
with receipts" — nobody's Trello card) and wrong to assume no project
underneath. The project is **the commission**: being on the hook, to
someone else, for a skill that has to be good. Every persona is the
same sentence with a different subject — first-timer (boss), cost-cutter
(CFO), skill-bank manager (the org), model-change (the vendor's
schedule).

## The two pivots that produced the opener

**Pivot 1 — scatter → entropy.** The scatter/schlep is real but it's
*some people's* Tuesday. What's true for everyone is the mutating
world: models change constantly, teammates mutate skills, runtimes
swap. But "you face risk" is a static condition — an insurance pitch
that only works on the already-scared. Pull needs the *moment* the risk
is felt.

**Pivot 2 — broke → weird.** "Your skill broke" imports the wrong
failure physics. A 404 is loud, binary, and blames itself. What agents
actually do is **quiet, graded, and blames the owner**: bad advice, a
wrong call, off-key in front of someone who matters. The kid at the
party — they did it, but everyone looks at you. Two properties make
this the opener:

- **It's the majority-felt form of the minority insight.** "Most skill
  authors don't comprehend procedural risk" (stable-but-wrong) — but
  every one of them has the "my agent did something weird" wince. Same
  fact, different altitude.
- **Inevitability beats recall.** "They will — that's the point;
  non-determinism is the job description" requires zero education and
  zero luck about who's been burned. It's a fact the buyer already
  holds.

The L+L in one sentence: **your dashboards catch errors, and weirdness
isn't an error.** Logs green, behavior off.

## Locked copy

**H1 (director-ruled this session):**

> Agents do unexpected things. That's the point. It's also YOUR
> problem.

**Sub candidates** (pick by out-loud test; H1 states the law, the sub
makes it this-week-in-front-of-someone):

1. *Your agent will do something weird this week. No crash, no error —
   just off. And it's on you.*
2. *Sometime this week, your agent will be weird in front of someone
   who matters. Nothing will error. And it's on you.*

**Category (new, load-bearing): commercial-grade skills.** Names the
work as hard and professional, gives the first-timer their door ("your
first commercial-grade skill"), and is a category claim no eval bench
can make.

**Supply that fits (the custody sentence):**

> Skillmaker Studio is where commercial-grade skills live — designed,
> versioned, measured in real agent sessions, and repaired when they
> drift. One surface, plain English, on the repo you already have. Not
> another eval bench in another tab: the desk the tabs were sitting on.

**How it fits (statistical honesty as the fit):**

> You can't make a non-deterministic thing predictable. You can measure
> it. Adopt the repo you already have — nothing moves. Our agents help
> you design evals that score behavior, not just uptime. Run it enough
> times to get a real number. When the number moves, you know first —
> and the trail back to why is right there.

**Mechanism (0:30 slot — where the procedural insight now lives):**

> The secret is the trail: every design choice pinned to the evidence
> behind it. When a skill is stable but not doing the job, you can see
> why — and fix the process, not just the prompt.

**Promise line (the honest inversion of "unbreakable"):**

> Nothing drifts silently. Nothing fails anonymously. Nothing stays
> wrong.

- *Silently* — receipts decay-check against the world; you're never the
  last to know.
- *Anonymously* — every number pinned to skill version + model; a
  mutated skill wearing your name resolves in one look ("the version I
  shipped passed 39/39 on the rated model; the thing that failed was
  someone's mutation on a model it was never rated for").
- *Stays wrong* — not just a verdict: an **operating table**, with the
  research trail to fix the process, not just poke the prompt.

**Fan-out list:**

> New model just shipped. You inherited skills you didn't write.
> Someone's asking "is this skill actually any good?" Your first
> commercial-grade skill is due. A teammate's version is wearing your
> skill's name.

**Closer:**

> We can't make your agent predictable — nobody can; that's what you
> hired it for. We can make sure nothing drifts silently, nothing fails
> anonymously, and nothing stays wrong. Free, local, phones nothing
> home. One command on the repo you already have.
>
> The only thing you risk is finding out.

## Positioning: custody, not scoring (vs. Braintrust)

Braintrust is the incumbent to beat as more-than-a-single-stack. Their
own quickstart (captured 2026-07-15) concedes the pitch:

1. **They demo the gap themselves.** Step 5 of their tutorial: "You
   might notice that some scores are 0%" — ExactMatch fails correct
   answers ("The movie is Se7en"). The first result of their first five
   minutes is a wrong number caused by a badly designed eval, and the
   fix is on you, in TypeScript. Deciding what "good" means is
   explicitly the user's problem. (Exactly the Alexandria finding:
   there's no smart process for picking the evals.)
2. **Their unit of test isn't the skill.** `task` is a TypeScript
   function you write calling a model API — a simulation you must build
   and keep in sync. The skill — the thing that misbehaves at the party
   — runs inside real claude-code/codex sessions. They measure a
   puppet; you vouch for the kid.
3. **Their on-ramp is a stack.** Sign up, two API keys, SDK, eval code,
   cloud UI, CI/CD wiring. And in the founder's own story, **Braintrust
   was literally one of the five tabs** — not the alternative to the
   schlep, a cause of it: the second copy, the separate surface.

The move is not "better evals" — that's their category and a losing
frame. The move is to change the question:

> An eval platform tells you the score. It can't tell you what to test,
> which version earned the number, why it drifted, or how to fix it.
> That's the other 90% of the job — and it's the part that was eating
> your week.

**The score is the easy part. Everything around the score is custody of
the skill.** Braintrust measures artifacts it doesn't own; Skillmaker
owns the artifact. Their architecture forbids the claim.

### The contrast block (Breathe Easy pattern — only pull-relevant rows)

| The question you're actually asking | Eval bench | Skillmaker |
|---|---|---|
| What should I even test? | You figure it out (in TypeScript) | Agents co-design evals with you, in plain English |
| Is the tested thing the shipped thing? | You maintain a synced copy | The skill itself is what runs — nothing to sync |
| Which version earned this number? | Experiment names, if you're disciplined | Pinned automatically: skill version + model |
| It drifted — why? | Here's the score delta | Here's the trail: what changed, and the research behind each choice |
| Now fix it | Back to your editor | The operating table is the same surface |

**Scoped-honesty guardrail:** we say out loud that Braintrust is
genuinely good at instrumenting API products with an eng team. Our
claim is narrower and therefore believable: *if you're on the hook for
skills that ride inside agents, a bench in another tab is the schlep,
not the solution.*

## Kills and relocations (recorded so they stay dead)

- **"Unbreakable / durable skills"** — killed as promise; overpromise
  with a countdown timer (skills drift, crumble, break — models change
  all the time). Inverted into the promise line: breakage *detected,
  attributed, repairable*.
- **The founder ad-line** ("it was faster to build Skillmaker than one
  skill") — killed for ad copy by the director: implies the product
  took an afternoon; reads as a lie. Survives only as internal origin
  story, above.
- **"They'd see the value once shown"** — director self-flagged as push
  talk. The procedural-risk insight it was carrying is *relocated*, not
  discarded: it lives in the 0:30 mechanism slot.
- **The kid-at-the-party metaphor** — conversations and maybe a blog
  post; not hero copy until it survives a live test. On a page it
  risks cute.
- **Broad→specific sequencing** — rejected. Specific moment first,
  fan-out second, broad frame earned last.
- **Scatter as the universal opener** — demoted to the commission
  vector's L+L. Real, testified, but not everyone's Tuesday.

skill's life. The contrast log, not this doc, picks the winner.

## Tests before this hardens

1. **The out-loud test.** Say the opener to the next three humans who
   ship skills or prompts; count winces vs. polite nods. Polite nods →
   back to the table.
2. **The agent→skill bridge seam.** The opener is agent-first; the
   product is skill-first. Does "my agent was weird" → "so I work on my
   skills" land without a push? Bridge line if needed: *the skill is
   the part of your agent you can actually operate on.*
3. **The first-timer side door.** The opener is tuned to the burned;
   the unburned (first commercial-grade skill due) enter through the
   fan-out and the commission card. Watch whether they actually do.
