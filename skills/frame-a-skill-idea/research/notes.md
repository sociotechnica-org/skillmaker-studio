# Research Notes — Frame A Skill Idea

## 1. What this skill does (restated from design.md)

The skill takes a director's raw idea for a skill and turns it into a
**researchable statement of intent** — one clear job worth building. The input
is an idea that may be too big, too thin, too fuzzy, or already solid. The
output is an idea shaped well enough that a researcher can act on it without
asking "what does this even do?" and a stranger could tell when to reach for
the finished skill.

The skill is judgment about *which move* to make on an incoming idea:
**Scope** it (harden / narrow), **Brainstorm** around it (grow / expand), or
**stand back** (the idea is already research-ready).

---

## 2. The core problem: ideas arrive in different broken states

Raw skill ideas fail to be researchable for distinct reasons. Recognizing
*which* reason matters, because the correct intervention differs:

| Idea state | What's wrong | Correct move |
|---|---|---|
| **Too big** | Multiple jobs tangled together; can't tell which one to build | **Scope** — decompose, pick one job |
| **Too thin** | A germ of an idea, not enough substance to research | **Brainstorm** — grow adjacent possibilities, flesh it out |
| **Too fuzzy** | Words are there but the core job is ambiguous or circular | **Scope** — sharpen the "when / who / what changes" |
| **Already solid** | Clear job, clear trigger, clear outcome | **Stand back** — pass through to research |

The skill must first *diagnose* which state applies, then apply the right
intervention, then verify the result meets the research-readiness bar.

## 3. Problem framing — the discipline behind "Scope"

### 3.1 Why problem definition matters

PMI research found over a third of projects fail because the problem was
poorly defined, and more than half of project dollars are wasted as a result.
Deciding on solutions before clearly defining the problem is one of the most
common and costly patterns. The counterintuitive truth: identifying the right
problem is harder than solving it.

Source: [Herrmann — Defining the Problem Before Solving It](https://www.thinkherrmann.com/whole-brain-thinking-blog/defining-the-problem-before-solving-it)

### 3.2 Problem framing techniques (relevant to Scope moves)

- **The 5 Whys** — repeatedly asking "Why?" to move past surface symptoms to
  root cause. Useful when the idea *sounds* specific but the underlying job is
  unclear.
- **The 5 W's** (Who, What, Why, Where, When) — forces multi-dimensional
  examination of the problem space. Maps well to skill-idea anatomy: Who
  reaches for this skill? What changes after they use it? When do they know
  they need it?
- **"How Might We" reframing** — transforms a problem statement into an
  open-ended question that invites solutions without over-specifying. Useful
  for testing whether a scoped idea is actionable.
- **Define in terms of needs, not solutions** — a principle from design
  thinking. If the idea describes a mechanism ("a skill that uses a decision
  tree…"), the job isn't defined yet.

Source: [Voltage Control — Problem Framing Best Practices](https://voltagecontrol.com/articles/problem-framing-in-design-thinking-best-practices/)

### 3.3 Scoping: narrowing without killing

An actionable problem statement must be:
- **Specific** — aims at a particular issue, not ocean-boiling.
- **Bounded** — has a timeframe, scope, or required accuracy.
- **Falsifiable** — you can tell whether the problem was solved.

The narrowing principle: "If you are tackling a large problem, choose one
slice of the problem pie to work on. Aim to narrow your focus to a single
chapter of that book."

Source: [Jonathan Rochelle — Narrow Your Problem Scope](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/narrow-your-problem-scope-turn-ideas-products-jonathan-rochelle)

### 3.4 Jobs-to-Be-Done (JTBD) as a scoping lens

JTBD defines a "job" as the progress a customer wants to make, stated without
reference to any specific solution. This is directly applicable: a
well-framed skill idea should state a job-to-be-done, not a mechanism.

Key JTBD practice: "Start by defining the scope: Are you trying to understand
a broad job like 'maintain a healthy lifestyle' or a more specific one like
'get a healthy meal on the table on a busy weeknight'?"

This maps to the skill's Scope move: help the director move from a broad job
to a specific, actionable one.

Source: [ProductPlan — JTBD Framework](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/jobs-to-be-done-framework/)

## 4. Brainstorming — the discipline behind "Grow"

### 4.1 When expansion is the right move

The Double Diamond model (Design Council, 2005) establishes that creative work
alternates between divergence (exploring broadly) and convergence (narrowing
to a decision). The first diamond diverges on *problems*, the second on
*solutions*.

For Frame-a-Skill-Idea, when the incoming idea is too thin, we are in the
divergent half of the first diamond: we need to expand the problem space
before we can converge on a researchable statement.

Source: [Double Diamond — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_Diamond_(design_process_model))

### 4.2 Techniques relevant to growing a thin idea

- **Mind mapping** — start with the seed idea at center, branch into adjacent
  possibilities, discover the job hiding inside.
- **SCAMPER** (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to another use,
  Eliminate, Reverse) — systematic prompts to mutate a thin idea into richer
  territory.
- **Reverse question** — "How would you make this problem worse?" — useful for
  surfacing what the idea is really trying to prevent or enable.
- **"Yes, and…"** building — take the director's seed and extend it
  constructively rather than critiquing it.

Source: [Asana — Brainstorming Techniques](https://asana.com/resources/brainstorming-techniques)

### 4.3 Guard rails during expansion

Divergent thinking must be time-boxed and followed by convergence. The risk in
the Brainstorm move is never converging back — producing a sprawling list
instead of a sharper idea. The skill should expand *then* converge to a single
researchable statement, not hand back a menu.

Source: [Ness Labs — Idea Evaluation](https://nesslabs.com/idea-evaluation)

## 5. The triage step: diagnosing the idea's state

### 5.1 Intake classification patterns

Triage systems (from IT ticket routing to medical intake) share a common
structure: assess the incoming item on a small number of dimensions, classify
it, then route it to the appropriate handler. The skill needs a lightweight
version of this for ideas.

Source: [AI Awareness — Intelligent Routing Rules](https://www.aiawareness.ai/define-intelligent-routing-rules-for-intake-triage/)

### 5.2 Diagnostic dimensions for a skill idea

Drawing from the problem-framing and JTBD literature, a skill idea can be
assessed on these dimensions:

1. **Job clarity** — Can you state the one job this skill does in a sentence
   that a stranger would understand? (If not → fuzzy or big)
2. **Trigger clarity** — Can you describe *when* someone would reach for it?
   (If not → fuzzy)
3. **Boundary clarity** — Can you say what this skill is *not* for? (If not →
   big)
4. **Substance** — Is there enough to the idea that a researcher could start
   investigating how to make it work? (If not → thin)

A simple decision tree:
- If the idea fails on substance → **Brainstorm** (grow it)
- If the idea fails on job, trigger, or boundary clarity → **Scope** (sharpen it)
- If the idea passes all four → **Stand back** (it's research-ready)
- If the idea fails on multiple dimensions → address the most fundamental
  gap first (substance before clarity)

### 5.3 Conversational elicitation

AI-driven requirements elicitation research shows that guided conversation —
asking follow-up questions, capturing details, generating clarifying questions
— is more effective than static templates for surfacing what stakeholders
actually mean.

Good elicitation asks about: edge cases, constraints, priorities, workflows,
exceptions, and success criteria. For idea framing specifically, the questions
are simpler: Who is this for? When do they need it? What changes after they
use it? What is this *not*?

Source: [DEV Community — AI Requirements Elicitation](https://dev.to/eliciteer/ai-requirements-elicitation-how-chatbot-interviews-can-fix-the-weakest-part-of-software-projects-53jf)

## 6. Research-readiness: the exit bar

### 6.1 What "research-ready" means for a skill idea

From the design.md: "a researcher can act on the idea without asking 'what
does this even do?', and a stranger could tell when to reach for the finished
skill."

This implies two tests:

1. **Researcher test** — Given only the framed idea, a researcher could begin
   investigating prior art, failure modes, and workflow without needing to
   come back and ask "but what is this?"
2. **Stranger test** — A person unfamiliar with the skill catalog, reading just
   the one-liner and trigger description, would know whether their current
   situation calls for this skill.

### 6.2 Quality criteria from adjacent fields

From the idea validation literature, a ready-to-act-on idea should have:
- A clear *who* (target user / persona)
- A clear *what* (the job / outcome)
- A clear *when* (the trigger / situation)
- A clear *not* (what's out of scope)
- Enough specificity that you could test whether the skill succeeded

Source: [IdeaWake — Idea Evaluation Process](https://ideawake.com/idea-evaluation-process-and-criteria/)

### 6.3 Teresa Torres's Opportunity Solution Tree (OST)

The OST starts with a desired outcome, branches into opportunities (customer
needs/pains), then maps solutions and assumption tests. The key insight for
this skill: "It turns big evergreen opportunities into smaller, more solvable
opportunities. And smaller opportunities lead to smaller solutions."

This is precisely the Scope move: take a big idea and decompose it into a
smaller, concrete opportunity that can be researched.

Source: [Product Talk — Opportunity Solution Trees](https://www.producttalk.org/opportunity-solution-trees/)

## 7. Failure modes and risks

### 7.1 Premature convergence

Settling on a framing before the idea space has been adequately explored. In
optimization theory, premature convergence means a system stabilizes on a
local minimum because it couldn't explore the full landscape. Applied here:
the Scope move fires too fast, and the idea gets narrowed to a job that isn't
the most valuable one.

**Mitigation:** The skill should ensure the director agrees with the chosen
framing, not just accept the first narrowing.

Source: [ByteStack Labs — Premature Convergence](https://medium.com/@bytestacklabs/premature-convergence-ea9fde1e88f2)

### 7.2 Over-specification

The framed idea crosses into design territory — specifying *how* the skill
should work rather than *what job* it does. The design.md is clear that this
skill is "not for settling how the skill works (research and drafting own
that)."

**Mitigation:** The output should be a statement of intent (job, trigger,
boundary), not a workflow or architecture.

### 7.3 Under-specification (rubber-stamping)

The skill declares an idea "research-ready" when it isn't, because it doesn't
probe deeply enough. The stranger test is the check: would a stranger know
when to use this?

**Mitigation:** Apply the four diagnostic dimensions (job, trigger, boundary,
substance) as a literal checklist before passing through.

### 7.4 Scope creep during brainstorming

The Brainstorm move generates exciting adjacent ideas but never converges back
to a single researchable statement. The director ends up with more confusion,
not less.

**Mitigation:** The Brainstorm move must end with convergence — "of these
possibilities, which one job should we frame?"

Source: [SafRan — Causes of Scope Creep](https://www.safran.com/blog/4-key-causes-of-scope-creep-and-how-to-mitigate-them)

### 7.5 Misdiagnosis

The skill routes to Scope when it should route to Brainstorm (or vice versa).
A too-thin idea gets narrowed further (making it thinner), or a too-big idea
gets expanded (making it bigger).

**Mitigation:** The diagnostic step needs to distinguish *thin* (lacks
substance) from *fuzzy/big* (has substance but lacks clarity/focus). Substance
is the discriminating factor.

### 7.6 Unnecessary intervention

The idea is already solid but the skill over-processes it — adding
unnecessary scoping or brainstorming that muddles a clear idea. The design.md
explicitly calls this out: the skill is "not for doing thinking the idea does
not need."

**Mitigation:** The "stand back" path must be a first-class outcome, not a
failure mode. The skill should *prefer* standing back if the four criteria are
met.

## 8. Analogous tools and prior art

### 8.1 The Double Diamond (Design Council)

Two cycles of diverge-then-converge. Frame-a-Skill-Idea lives in the first
half of the first diamond: making sure the problem (the skill's job) is well
defined before any solution design begins.

### 8.2 Opportunity Solution Trees (Teresa Torres)

Hierarchical decomposition of broad outcomes into specific opportunities. The
Scope move is structurally identical: break a big idea into smaller, more
concrete opportunities and pick one.

### 8.3 Problem Statement Workshops

Design teams routinely run sessions where a vague brief is turned into a
sharp problem statement through structured questioning. The skill automates a
lightweight version of this.

### 8.4 JTBD Interviews

The JTBD tradition of interviewing customers about the "job" they're hiring a
product for maps directly to interviewing the director about the "job" their
skill idea would do.

## 9. Design implications (for workflow section of design.md)

Based on this research, the workflow should likely follow this structure:

1. **Receive** the raw idea (from bundle.json or director input).
2. **Diagnose** — assess on the four dimensions (job clarity, trigger clarity,
   boundary clarity, substance). This is the triage step.
3. **Route** to the appropriate move:
   - Substance is low → **Brainstorm** move
   - Clarity is low (but substance exists) → **Scope** move
   - All dimensions pass → **Stand back**
4. **Execute** the chosen move:
   - **Brainstorm**: expand with targeted questions or generative prompts,
     then converge to a single candidate job statement.
   - **Scope**: ask sharpening questions (Who? When? What changes? What's
     out?), decompose if needed, converge to one job.
   - **Stand back**: confirm the idea meets the bar and pass it through.
5. **Verify** — run the researcher test and stranger test on the output.
6. **Deliver** a researchable statement of intent: the one job, its trigger,
   and its boundary.

Key constraints:
- The output must not cross into design territory (no "how it works").
- The Brainstorm move must converge; it cannot hand back a menu.
- The Stand-back path must be a proud first-class outcome.
- The skill should prefer minimal intervention (don't over-process solid ideas).
